## Price for the yes you can get today

There's a version of pricing strategy that lives on whiteboards, and a version that lives in your buyer's head. Founders spend most of their time on the first one. The second is the only one that closes deals.

Here's what I keep seeing. A founder reads that the best SaaS companies charge based on usage — per API call, per seat-hour, per gigabyte. It feels modern. It feels like you're capturing the value you create. So they build it into the launch.

Then the deals slow down, and nobody can say why.

The why is simple: usage-based pricing asks the buyer to do a forecast. To say yes, they have to predict how much of your product they'll use — before they've used it, before they trust it, before they even know if it works for their team. That's a hard ask for a product with a track record. For a pre-PMF startup, it's nearly impossible. So the buyer does the rational thing and waits. "Let me see how this goes first." Your sales cycle just doubled.

A seat-based tier model does the opposite. Three tiers, a price per seat, a line they can read in five seconds. The buyer doesn't need a spreadsheet to get budget approval. They need a number they can defend to their boss, and "twelve seats at X" is a number you can defend. You removed the math, so you removed the hesitation.

The free tier matters here too, but only if it has a job. A free tier isn't generosity — it's the on-ramp. It exists to get the product into the workflow so the upgrade trigger can fire on its own. If you can't name the moment a free user becomes a paid one, your free tier is a cost center, not a funnel.

None of this means usage-based pricing is wrong. It means it's a *later* decision. Once people trust the product, once they can predict their own usage because they've lived with it, usage-based becomes the best expansion engine there is. You charge more as customers get more value, and nobody flinches. But that's a scale move. You earn it after the first hundred customers, not before the first ten.

So the sequence is: get the yes you can get today, then upgrade the model once trust is in the bank. Simple tiers to land them. Usage-based to grow them. Don't run the second play before you've won the first.

If you're pricing a launch right now, ask one question: can my buyer say yes to this without opening a spreadsheet? If the answer is no, you don't have a pricing problem. You have a friction problem wearing a pricing costume.

Charge in a way they can approve today. Get clever once they're already in.
